# CUSTOMER_PERSONAS.md
> Internal document — use when designing features, writing copy, preparing demos or training sales
> These are fictional but realistic representations of real people ILS serves.

---

## Why Personas Matter

ILS serves very different people with very different needs.

A Programme Manager cares about accreditation and quality.
A Teacher cares about reducing workload and knowing their students.
A Student cares about clarity and progress.

If we design, market and sell as if they are the same person — we lose all of them.

These personas ensure every decision — product, marketing, sales — is made with a specific person in mind.

## Persona 1 — The Programme Manager (Buyer)

**Name:** Sandra van der Berg
**Role:** Programme Manager, HBO Business Administration
**Institution:** Medium-sized HBO institution, 800 students in her programme
**Age:** 48
**Reports to:** Dean of the Faculty

---

### Her World

Sandra is responsible for everything that happens inside her programme. Curriculum quality, teacher performance, student success rates, accreditation readiness. She manages 24 teachers across 6 cohorts. She spends a significant part of her week in meetings — with teachers, with the quality team, with the board.

Every four to six years, her programme goes through accreditation. That process is stressful and time-consuming because the evidence is scattered across dozens of systems, Excel files and email threads.

She does not have time to learn complex software. She needs insight, not dashboards.

---

### Goals
- Maintain programme quality and NVAO accreditation readiness at all times
- Reduce student dropout — her programme's dropout rate is too high in year 1
- Have real-time visibility into how the programme is performing
- Spend less time collecting evidence and more time acting on it
- Give teachers the tools they need to work effectively

### Pain Points
- Curriculum exists in Word documents that are disconnected from actual teaching
- She discovers student problems too late — usually after a teacher raises it manually
- Accreditation preparation takes months of collecting evidence that should already exist
- She cannot see, at a glance, whether learning outcomes are being assessed consistently
- Her teachers use different tools, different grade scales, different rubrics — there is no consistency

### What Success Looks Like for Sandra
- She can open one dashboard and see the health of her entire programme
- When accreditation comes, the evidence is already collected — no panic preparation
- Student dropout in year 1 decreases measurably
- Teachers are working from the same curriculum, the same rubrics, the same standards

### What Excites Her About ILS
- A single platform that connects curriculum, teaching and outcomes
- Real-time programme health — not an annual report
- The accreditation argument — evidence collected continuously
- Coordinator tools that identify risk before students fail

### What Worries Her About ILS
- Implementation effort — she cannot afford to disrupt her programme mid-year
- Teacher adoption — she has experienced resistance to new tools before
- Data migration — years of curriculum work in Word and Excel
- Cost justification to the board

### How to Sell to Sandra
Lead with outcomes, not features. Show her the programme health dashboard first. Tell her the accreditation story. Give her a concrete implementation timeline. Address the teacher adoption question before she asks it.

## Persona 2 — The Teacher (User)

**Name:** Martijn Jacobs
**Role:** Lecturer, HRM and Organisational Behaviour
**Institution:** Same HBO institution as Sandra
**Age:** 36
**Classes:** 3 active classes, 78 students total

---

### His World

Martijn loves teaching. He does not love administration. He spends several hours each week on things that have nothing to do with teaching — entering grades in one system, tracking attendance in another, looking up submission deadlines in a third. He has a WhatsApp group with students because communication in the official system is too cumbersome.

He cares deeply about his students but loses track of individuals when class sizes exceed 25. He does not always know which students are struggling until they approach him — or until it is too late.

He is not against new software. He is against software that creates more work without solving a real problem.

---

### Goals
- Spend more time teaching and less time on administration
- Know which students need attention before problems escalate
- Give students useful, consistent feedback — not just grades
- Have a clear picture of what he is supposed to teach and how it connects to outcomes

### Pain Points
- Too many systems — curriculum in one place, grades in another, attendance in a third
- No connection between his grading and the curriculum outcomes — he grades but does not know if the learning happened
- Preparing rubrics from scratch for every assignment
- No visibility into individual student progress across the whole module
- Inconsistency — every teacher does things slightly differently, creating confusion for students

### What Success Looks Like for Martijn
- He opens ILS at the start of the week and sees everything he needs — his classes, his schedule, his assessments
- Grading is connected to rubric criteria that connect to outcomes — he is not just assigning numbers
- He can see, at a glance, which students are at risk across all his classes
- Students receive structured feedback, not just a score

### What Excites Him About ILS
- Lesson planning and grading in one place
- Rubric-based assessment that is already connected to the curriculum
- Student cockpit — seeing risk signals without having to hunt for them
- Less time on administration

### What Worries Him About ILS
- Learning curve — he is already busy
- Will this replace the way he works, or add to it?
- What happens to his existing grade records and curriculum materials?
- Will students actually use it?

### How to Sell to Martijn
Do not sell to Martijn — support him. Show him how ILS reduces his workload in the first week. The grading workflow. The attendance in one click. The rubric already built. If he sees time saved in week one, he becomes an advocate.

## Persona 3 — The Curriculum Designer (User)

**Name:** Lena Horstmann
**Role:** Curriculum Developer, Educational Design Team
**Institution:** Large HBO institution with a central educational design department
**Age:** 41

---

### Her World

Lena designs programmes. She works with programme managers and teachers to translate educational goals into concrete curriculum structures — outcomes, modules, assignments, assessment frameworks. She is methodical, detail-oriented and passionate about learning design.

Her biggest frustration is that her work disappears into Word documents. She designs a beautiful curriculum with aligned outcomes and rubrics — and then watches it implemented inconsistently by 15 different teachers. She has no way to know whether her design actually worked.

---

### Goals
- Create curriculum that is coherent, outcome-aligned and actually usable by teachers
- See whether her curriculum design produces the intended learning outcomes in practice
- Ensure rubrics and criteria are applied consistently across all teachers
- Reduce the time gap between curriculum design and classroom implementation

### Pain Points
- Curriculum documents are static — they do not connect to actual teaching or assessment
- No feedback loop — she designs, it gets implemented (or not), she never knows the result
- Rubrics are redesigned by every teacher — consistency is lost
- Outcome mapping exists on paper but is not enforced in practice

### What Success Looks Like for Lena
- She can see, in real time, whether learning outcomes are being assessed in the way she designed
- Rubrics she creates in ILS are used directly by teachers — no reinvention
- The curriculum health dashboard shows her where the gaps are between design and delivery
- She can use outcome data to improve curriculum in the next iteration

### What Excites Her About ILS
- Curriculum Composer — designing in a live connected system
- Outcome mapping that connects design to assessment
- Rubric library shared across all teachers
- Curriculum effectiveness dashboard — closing the feedback loop

### What Worries Her About ILS
- Will teachers use the rubrics she creates, or work around them?
- Is the system flexible enough for complex curriculum structures?
- Data migration of existing curriculum frameworks

### How to Sell to Lena
Show her the curriculum effectiveness view first — the feedback loop she has always wanted. Then show how rubrics flow from design to teacher to grading to outcome evidence. This is the dream she has been trying to achieve manually.

## Persona 4 — The Student (User)

**Name:** Amir Benali
**Role:** Year 2 student, HBO Business Administration
**Institution:** Same programme as Sandra and Martijn
**Age:** 20

---

### His World

Amir is trying to figure out how to succeed. He attends most lectures but loses track of deadlines. He receives grades but does not always understand what they mean or what he should do differently. He has asked a teacher for feedback twice and received a number both times.

He is not struggling — but he is not flourishing either. He is somewhere in the middle, where students tend to stay invisible until something goes wrong.

---

### Goals
- Know what he needs to do and when
- Understand what is expected of him before he submits work, not after
- See whether he is on track to pass the year
- Get useful feedback that helps him improve

### Pain Points
- Deadlines are scattered across emails, WhatsApp groups and different systems
- He receives grades but no clear picture of what they mean for his progression
- Rubrics are vague or inconsistent — he does not know what "good" looks like
- He cannot easily see whether he is at risk of failing until it is too late

### What Success Looks Like for Amir
- One place where he can see all his assignments, deadlines and grades
- A clear picture of his progress toward competencies — not just scores
- Feedback that tells him specifically what to improve
- An early warning if he is at risk — before it becomes a formal problem

### What Excites Him About ILS
- Dashboard that shows everything in one place
- Outcome progress — he can see what competencies he is developing
- Clear rubric criteria before submission — he knows what is expected
- Feedback tied to specific criteria

### What Worries Him About ILS
- Another system to learn
- Will teachers actually use it consistently?

### How to Involve Amir
Amir does not buy ILS — but he determines whether it succeeds. If students find ILS useful in week one, adoption is permanent. If it feels like extra work, they abandon it. The student experience must be simple, clear and immediately valuable.

## Persona 5 — The Educational Advisor / SLB (User)

**Name:** Fatima el-Haddad
**Role:** Student Learning and Career Advisor (Studieloopbaanbegeleider)
**Institution:** Same HBO institution
**Age:** 34
**Advisees:** 45 students across two cohorts

---

### Her World

Fatima is responsible for the personal and academic development of 45 students. She has scheduled check-in meetings every 6–8 weeks, but most of what she does is reactive — students come to her when something has already gone wrong. She spends a lot of time searching for information — grades in one system, attendance in another — before she can have a meaningful conversation.

The BSA (Binding Study Advice) process is the most stressful part of her year. Collecting evidence for 45 students, assessing whether they meet the progression requirements, writing formal advice — all done manually.

---

### Goals
- Have a complete picture of each student before every meeting
- Identify students who need support before they reach crisis point
- Make the BSA process evidence-based and defensible
- Spend less time collecting information and more time supporting students

### Pain Points
- Student information is scattered across multiple systems
- She cannot see attendance, grades and outcome progress in one place
- BSA decisions are made with incomplete information
- She finds out about struggling students too late — usually from the student themselves
- The formal BSA process is time-consuming and document-heavy

### What Success Looks Like for Fatima
- She opens a student profile and sees attendance, grades, outcomes and risk signals in one view
- BSA evidence is collected automatically — she reviews and decides, she does not collect
- She can see which of her 45 students needs attention this week — without manually checking each one
- Students arrive at meetings already knowing their progress — the conversation is more productive

### What Excites Her About ILS
- Student profile with full picture — attendance + grades + outcomes + risk
- BSA dashboard — evidence collected, not hunted
- Risk signals — she knows before the student does
- Progression review workflow — structured, documented, defensible

### What Worries Her About ILS
- Will she trust the data? What if it is incomplete?
- Privacy — she handles sensitive student information
- Learning the new system

### How to Sell to Fatima
Show her the student profile page. Then show her the BSA dashboard. Ask her: "How long does BSA preparation currently take?" Then show her how ILS makes it a review process instead of a collection process.

## Persona 6 — The ICT Manager (Gatekeeper / Buyer)

**Name:** Peter Smits
**Role:** Manager ICT & Digital Infrastructure
**Institution:** Same HBO institution
**Age:** 52

---

### His World

Peter is not an educator. He is responsible for the technical infrastructure of the institution — servers, software, security, integrations. Every new software request lands on his desk. He has seen too many projects that were launched with enthusiasm and abandoned within a year because adoption was poor or integration was impossible.

He is not against ILS. He is cautious. He wants answers before he says yes.

---

### Goals
- Ensure all software meets GDPR and institutional security requirements
- Minimise integration complexity with existing systems (SIS, Microsoft 365, SSO)
- Prevent tool sprawl — the institution already has too many systems
- Ensure any new platform has a realistic adoption plan and support structure

### Pain Points
- Vendors oversell and underdeliver on integration promises
- GDPR compliance is his responsibility — he cannot afford a breach
- He manages 20+ software licenses already — he is not enthusiastic about adding more
- Poor adoption means wasted budget and his team ends up supporting a tool no one uses

### What Success Looks Like for Peter
- ILS integrates cleanly with Microsoft 365 and the institution's SIS
- GDPR documentation is complete and he can sign the DPA with confidence
- Adoption is high — he is not managing a ghost system
- Support is reliable — if something breaks, someone answers

### What Excites Him About ILS
- Potential to replace multiple tools with one (reduces complexity)
- Modern architecture — not a legacy system
- European hosting and GDPR compliance built-in

### What Worries Him About ILS
- Single sign-on (SSO) — does it integrate with Microsoft/Azure AD?
- Data ownership — where is data stored? Who can access it?
- What happens if the institution wants to leave ILS? Can they export their data?
- SLA — what is the uptime guarantee? What is the support response time?

### How to Sell to Peter
Answer his questions before he asks them. Have a security and GDPR page ready. Have a Data Processing Agreement template ready. Explain the integration roadmap honestly. Peter does not need to love ILS — he needs to not block it.

## How to Use These Personas

**When designing a feature:** Ask — which persona does this serve? What is their pain point? Would they notice this feature in week one?

**When writing website copy:** Ask — which persona is reading this page? What do they care about? Use their language.

**When preparing a demo:** Ask — who is in the room? If Sandra is there, lead with programme health. If Martijn is there, lead with the grading workflow. If Peter is there, lead with security.

**When prioritizing the roadmap:** Ask — which persona is blocked by this gap? How many of them exist in our customer base?
